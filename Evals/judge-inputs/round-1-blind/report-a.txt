Comprehensive Inventory of Board of Directors Obligations Applicable to Category I Global Systemically Important Bank Holding Companies and Their Insured Depository Institution SubsidiariesMay 29, 2026PRIVILEGED & CONFIDENTIALI. IntroductionCategory I global systemically important bank holding companies (ﾒGSIBsﾓ) occupy a unique position in the American financial regulatory architecture. As the largest, most complex, and most systemically consequential banking organizations operating in the United States, they are subject to the most stringent tier of enhanced prudential standards under federal law. The boards of directors of these institutions, and of their insured depository institution (ﾒIDIﾓ) subsidiaries, bear governance obligations that are unmatched in their breadth, granularity, and cumulative weight.These obligations did not arise from a single, coherent regulatory design. They accumulated over decades, across multiple federal agencies exercising overlapping jurisdiction, in response to successive financial crises and evolving supervisory philosophies. The result is a governance framework of extraordinary density: board-level duties are embedded in federal statutes, codified regulations, interagency guidelines, agency-specific supervisory guidance, examination manuals, and supervisory letters, each of which carries a distinct legal character and enforcement posture, but all of which are, for practical purposes, simultaneously operative.This memorandum provides a comprehensive, citation-level inventory of all current, in-force obligations imposed on the boards of directors of Category I GSIB holding companies and their IDI subsidiaries. The inventory draws from all applicable federal regulations and agency guidance issued by the Federal Reserve, the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency (ﾒOCCﾓ), the Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation (ﾒFDICﾓ), the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (ﾒCFPBﾓ), and the Securities and Exchange Commission (ﾒSECﾓ), as well as relevant interagency standards. It captures obligations at every level of board structure: the full board, specific committees (risk, audit, compensation, and nominating/governance), and any distinctions between inside and outside or independent and non-independent directors.The purpose of this inventory is to enable the identification of ﾒpolicy sludge,ﾓ duplicative, overlapping, and unduly burdensome requirements that have accumulated over time, and to serve as a working policy reform document. Each obligation is traced to its precise regulatory source, its nature is described, and overlaps with parallel requirements from other sources are identified. The memorandum concludes with a synthesis of the reform implications that emerge from this exercise.As of the date of this memorandum, eight U.S. bank holding companies are designated as GSIBs: JPMorgan Chase & Co., Bank of America Corporation, Citigroup Inc., Goldman Sachs Group, Inc., Morgan Stanley, Wells Fargo & Company, The Bank of New York Mellon Corporation, and State Street Corporation. (Federal Register, Regulatory Capital Rule: Risk-Based Capital Surcharges for GSIBs)II. Executive SummaryThis memorandum identifies and catalogues board-level obligations for Category I GSIBs across more than twenty regulatory domains, drawing from four distinct layers of authority: federal statute, federal regulation, supervisory guidance, and supervisory rating systems. The key findings are as follows:Multi-Agency Layering. Category I GSIB boards are simultaneously subject to the Federal Reserveﾕs enhanced prudential standards (12 C.F.R. Part 252), the OCCﾕs Heightened Standards (Appendix D to 12 C.F.R. Part 30), the FDICﾕs safety and soundness standards and proposed corporate governance guidelines (12 C.F.R. Part 364), interagency guidance, and SEC disclosure requirements. Each agency has independently issued governance-related requirements that frequently address the same substantive governance concerns through different textual formulations, different periodicity requirements, and different enforcement mechanisms.Scope of Obligations. Board-level obligations span every major regulatory domain: strategy and risk appetite; board composition and independence; risk committee structure; audit committee structure; compensation governance; nominating/governance committee functions; capital planning and stress testing; liquidity risk management; resolution planning; recovery planning; operational resilience; model risk governance; third-party risk management; cybersecurity governance; BSA/AML program oversight; consumer compliance oversight; safety and soundness standards generally; conflicts of interest and insider transactions; senior management accountability; group structure governance; and whistleblower programs, corporate culture, and ethical conduct.Significant Overlap. The inventory reveals pervasive duplication, particularly between the Federal Reserveﾕs holding company-level requirements and the OCCﾕs Heightened Standards at the bank subsidiary level. Materially identical obligations, such as the requirement to approve a risk appetite statement, approve the risk governance framework, maintain a board risk committee, and oversee senior management accountability, appear in parallel instruments from different agencies applied at different levels of the corporate structure.The Guidance Problem. A substantial portion of the most operationally significant board obligations arises from supervisory guidance rather than binding regulation. While federal banking agencies have acknowledged that guidance does not have the force of law, in practice, noncompliance with guidance at Category I GSIBs generates supervisory criticism, adverse examination findings, and potential predicates for formal enforcement action.Reform Opportunities. The inventory identifies specific, actionable reform opportunities, including cross-agency harmonization of holding company and bank subsidiary governance requirements, consolidation of domain-specific board approval requirements, rationalization of overlapping guidance documents, and resolution of inconsistent periodicity requirements across agencies.III. Foundational Framework: Regulatory Architecture and General Board DutiesA. Statutory ArchitectureThe enhanced prudential standards regime applicable to Category I GSIBs originates in Section 165 of the Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act, codified at 12 U.S.C. Section 5365. Congress directed the Board of Governors of the Federal Reserve System to establish prudential standards for bank holding companies with total consolidated assets of $250 billion or more that are ﾒmore stringent than the standards and requirements applicable to nonbank financial companies and bank holding companies that do not present similar risks to the financial stability of the United States.ﾓ (12 U.S.C. Section 5365)Section 165(b)(1)(A) mandates five categories of prudential standards: (1) risk-based capital requirements and leverage limits; (2) liquidity requirements; (3) overall risk management requirements; (4) resolution plan requirements; and (5) concentration limits. Each generates a distinct domain of board-level oversight obligations. Section 165(h) separately mandates that each publicly traded bank holding company with $50 billion or more in total consolidated assets establish a board-level risk committee responsible for enterprise-wide risk management oversight, including at least one risk management expert. (12 U.S.C. Section 5365)The Bank Holding Company Act provides the primary authority for Federal Reserve supervision of GSIB holding companies. Section 5 of the BHCA, codified at 12 U.S.C. Section 1844, authorizes the Board of Governors to issue regulations governing bank holding company activities, require financial condition and risk management reports, conduct examinations, and order termination of activities that ﾒconstitute a serious risk to the financial safety, soundness, or stability of a subsidiary bank.ﾓ (12 U.S.C. Section 1844)Section 39 of the Federal Deposit Insurance Act, codified at 12 U.S.C. Section 1831p-1, authorizes the federal banking agencies to prescribe safety and soundness standards for insured depository institutions, covering internal controls, loan documentation, credit underwriting, interest rate exposure, asset growth, and compensation. (12 U.S.C. Section 1831p-1)B. The Federal Reserveﾕs Consolidated Supervision FrameworkSR Letter 12-17/CA 12-14 establishes the overarching supervisory architecture for Category I GSIBs and Large Institution Supervision Coordinating Committee (ﾒLISCCﾓ) firms. It identifies two primary supervisory objectives: (1) enhancing resiliency to lower the probability of failure; and (2) reducing the impact on the financial system in the event of failure or material weakness. (SR 12-17/CA 12-14, Consolidated Supervision Framework for Large Financial Institutions)Under SR 12-17, the board of directors and its committees should: (a) maintain a clearly articulated corporate strategy and institutional risk appetite; (b) ensure senior management has the expertise to manage core business lines and critical operations; (c) maintain a corporate culture emphasizing compliance with laws and regulations, consumer protection, and avoidance of conflicts of interest; (d) ensure the effectiveness and independence of internal audit, compliance, and risk management functions; (e) assign senior managers responsibility for aligning investments with corporate strategies; and (f) ensure that management information systems support board responsibilities. (SR 12-17/CA 12-14)C. SR 21-3: Board Effectiveness GuidanceSR Letter 21-3/CA 21-1 is the governing supervisory guidance on board effectiveness for bank holding companies with $100 billion or more in total consolidated assets. It identifies five key attributes of an effective board: (1) set clear, aligned, and consistent direction regarding strategy and risk appetite; (2) direct senior management regarding the boardﾕs information needs; (3) oversee and hold senior management accountable; (4) support the independence and stature of independent risk management and internal audit; and (5) maintain a capable board composition and governance structure. (SR 21-3/CA 21-1, Supervisory Guidance on Board of Directorsﾕ Effectiveness)SR 21-3 is supervisory guidance, not a regulation with the force of law. However, it informs the Federal Reserveﾕs LFI (Large Financial Institution) rating system, which assigns three component ratings: Capital Planning and Positions, Liquidity Risk Management and Positions, and Governance and Controls. Board effectiveness is a discrete sub-element within Governance and Controls. According to Debevoise & Plimptonﾕs analysis, the wording of the five board effectiveness attributes in SR 21-3 is ﾒnearly identicalﾓ to that of the board effectiveness element of the LFI rating system. A deficient rating in any component severely impairs an institutionﾕs ability to engage in new activities or pursue acquisitions. (Debevoise & Plimpton, Federal Reserve Board Finalizes Board Effectiveness Guidance)D. OCC Heightened StandardsThe OCCﾕs Heightened Standards, Appendix D to 12 C.F.R. Part 30, currently apply to covered banks with average total consolidated assets of $50 billion or more, encompassing all Category I GSIB national bank subsidiaries. They establish minimum standards for the design and implementation of a covered bankﾕs risk governance framework and minimum standards for board oversight of that framework. (OCC Appendix D to 12 C.F.R. Part 30)The OCC has proposed raising the threshold to $700 billion (OCC Bulletin 2025-51), which would limit the guidelines to approximately eight institutions, while also soliciting comment on whether to rescind the guidelines entirely and reissue them as nonbinding guidance, and whether to remove or adjust standards that duplicate the Federal Reserveﾕs Enhanced Prudential Standards. (OCC Bulletin 2025-51, OCC Guidelines Establishing Heightened Standards: Notice of Proposed Rulemaking)The American Bankers Associationﾕs comment letter on this proposal explicitly argued that the Heightened Standards establish ﾒoverly prescriptive standards for banking organizationsﾕ design and implementation of a risk governance framework and how boards of directors must carry out their oversight responsibilities,ﾓ and recommended complete rescission. The ABAﾕs core argument is that OCC-supervised institutions with holding companies exceeding $50 billion remain subject to the Federal Reserveﾕs Enhanced Prudential Standards, rendering the OCCﾕs standards duplicative. (ABA Letter to OCC on Heightened Standards Proposal) Note: The ABA is a trade association representing large bank members; this letter reflects an industry advocacy position. However, the OCCﾕs own proposed rulemaking acknowledges the duplication concern, lending independent regulatory support to the structural argument.E. Source of Strength and Foundational Holding Company DutiesThe bedrock governance obligation is the source of strength doctrine, codified at 12 C.F.R. Section 225.4(a)(1): ﾒA bank holding company shall serve as a source of financial and managerial strength to its subsidiary banks and shall not conduct its operations in an unsafe or unsound manner.ﾓ (12 C.F.R. Section 225.4) This obligation conditions the boardﾕs strategic decision-making authority: any strategy that would impair the parentﾕs ability to support its banking subsidiaries is potentially unsafe or unsound.IV. Full Board Obligations: Strategy, Risk Appetite, and Governance FrameworkA. Strategic PlanningUnder SR 21-3, an effective board oversees the development of, reviews, approves, and periodically monitors the firmﾕs strategy and risk appetite. Strategy and risk appetite must be ﾒdeveloped, reviewed, and approved consistent with one another.ﾓ (SR 21-3/CA 21-1 Attachment)The OCCﾕs Heightened Standards require the board to evaluate and approve the strategic plan and monitor implementation at least annually. The plan must cover at minimum a three-year period, contain a comprehensive risk assessment, articulate strategic objectives, explain how the risk governance framework will be updated, and be reviewed and updated as necessary. (OCC Appendix D to 12 C.F.R. Part 30)The FDICﾕs proposed Guidelines (Appendix C to Part 364) impose structurally parallel requirements with substantively similar content. (FDIC Proposed Guidelines, Appendix C to Part 364) Note: The FDICﾕs proposed Guidelines have not been finalized and do not currently carry the force of binding law.Overlap observation: For a Category I GSIB with a national bank subsidiary, the strategic planning obligation arises under Federal Reserve guidance (holding company level), OCC Heightened Standards (bank level), and, prospectively, FDIC proposed Guidelines (if the firm also has an FDIC-supervised subsidiary). Each is technically a distinct obligation running to a different legal entity and regulator.B. Risk Appetite StatementThe OCCﾕs Heightened Standards require a comprehensive written risk appetite statement that serves as the basis for the risk governance framework, including both qualitative components (describing a safe and sound risk culture) and quantitative limits (incorporating stress testing and addressing earnings, capital, and liquidity). The board or board risk committee must review and approve the risk appetite statement at least annually. (OCC Appendix D to 12 C.F.R. Part 30)SR 21-3 defines risk appetite as ﾒthe aggregate level and types of risk the board and senior management are willing to assume to achieve the firmﾕs strategic business objectives, consistent with applicable capital, liquidity, and other requirements and constraints.ﾓ A clear risk appetite includes sufficient detail to enable the chief risk officer and independent risk management to set firm-wide risk limits. (SR 21-3/CA 21-1 Attachment)The FDICﾕs proposed Guidelines require at least quarterly board or risk committee review of the risk appetite statement, a materially different periodicity than the OCCﾕs annual minimum. (FDIC Proposed Guidelines, Appendix C to Part 364)Divergent periodicity: This is a concrete example of regulatory friction: the OCC requires annual review; the FDIC would require quarterly review. A GSIB with subsidiaries subject to both regimes would need to manage different review cycles for conceptually the same document.C. Risk Governance FrameworkThe OCCﾕs Heightened Standards require the board or board risk committee to approve the risk governance framework and any significant changes to it, and to monitor compliance with the framework. The framework must cover all risk categories applicable to the covered bank and include policies and supporting processes for effectively identifying, measuring, monitoring, and controlling risks. (OCC Appendix D to 12 C.F.R. Part 30)SR 21-3 states that an effective board reviews and approves significant policies, programs, and plans based on the firmﾕs strategy, risk appetite, risk management capacity, and structure. These include, among others: the capital plan, recovery and resolution plans, audit plan, enterprise-wide risk management policies, liquidity risk management policies, compliance risk management program, and performance management and compensation programs. (SR 21-3/CA 21-1 Attachment)The OCC permits a covered bank to use its parent companyﾕs risk governance framework ﾒin its entirety, without modificationﾓ if: the framework meets Appendix Dﾕs minimum standards; the risk profiles of the parent and the bank are substantially the same; and the bank has demonstrated this through a documented assessment conducted at least annually. When risk profiles are not substantially the same, the bank must establish its own framework to ensure its risk profile is ﾒeasily distinguished and separate from that of its parent.ﾓ (OCC Appendix D to 12 C.F.R. Part 30)D. Board Self-AssessmentThe OCC Heightened Standards require an annual self-assessment evaluating board effectiveness. (OCC Appendix D to 12 C.F.R. Part 30) SR 21-3 similarly states that an effective board evaluates its strengths and weaknesses on an ongoing basis, including committee performance. Notably, the final SR 21-3 removed a provision from the 2017 proposal that would have suggested providing examiners with self-assessment results. (SR 21-3/CA 21-1 Attachment)V. Board Composition, Independence, and Governance StructureA. Director IndependenceThe OCCﾕs Heightened Standards require at least two board members to satisfy independence criteria: (1) not an officer or employee of the parent or bank (and not having been one in the prior three years); (2) not a member of the immediate family of a current or recent executive officer; and (3) qualifying as independent under exchange listing standards. (OCC Appendix D to 12 C.F.R. Part 30)The FDICﾕs proposed Guidelines would require a majority of outside and independent directors, using a different definitional framework. (FDIC Proposed Guidelines, Appendix C to Part 364)The Federal Reserveﾕs SR 21-3 does not impose a quantitative independence threshold but evaluates independence qualitatively through the LFI rating systemﾕs Governance and Controls component. (SR 21-3/CA 21-1 Attachment)Three divergent regimes: A single GSIB enterprise may be simultaneously subject to the OCCﾕs two-director minimum, the FDICﾕs proposed majority requirement, and the Federal Reserveﾕs qualitative standard, each with different definitions, thresholds, and enforcement mechanisms.B. Director Nominee Selection and DiversitySR 21-3 requires that the board establish a process to identify and select nominees with ﾒa mix of skills, knowledge, experience, and perspectives,ﾓ considering expertise, availability, integrity, potential conflicts of interest, and ﾒa diverse pool of potential nominees, including women and minorities.ﾓ (SR 21-3/CA 21-1 Attachment)According to Covington & Burlingﾕs analysis, this language suggests the Federal Reserve may formally consider, when rating a financial institution under the LFI rating system, the extent to which a board fails to consider a diverse pool of nominees. This is a law firmﾕs interpretation, not a statement of binding regulatory authority. (Covington & Burling, Prepare to Meet New Fed Expectations on Effective Boards)SEC Regulation S-K Item 407(c) requires disclosure of whether and how the nominating committee considers diversity in identifying nominees, and if a diversity policy exists, how it is implemented and how effectiveness is assessed. (Reg S-K Rule 229.407)C. Director TrainingThe OCC Heightened Standards require a formal, ongoing training program for all directors, covering complex products and risks, applicable laws and regulations, and other topics identified by the board. (OCC Appendix D to 12 C.F.R. Part 30) The FDIC proposed Guidelines impose a substantively identical training obligation. (FDIC Proposed Guidelines, Appendix C to Part 364) SR 21-3 does not impose a formal training requirement but addresses director knowledge as a qualitative attribute of board effectiveness.VI. Board Committee ObligationsA. Risk CommitteeThe Federal Reserveﾕs Regulation YY (12 C.F.R. Section 252.33) requires each Category I GSIB to maintain a board-level risk committee responsible for enterprise-wide risk management oversight. The risk committee must include at least one member with experience in risk management of large, complex firms, and must meet at least quarterly. (12 U.S.C. Section 5365; SR 21-3/CA 21-1 Attachment)SR 21-3 states that an effective risk committee supports the independence of independent risk management by communicating directly with the chief risk officer, overseeing the appropriateness of independent risk managementﾕs budget and staffing, and providing independent risk management with direct and unrestricted access to the risk committee. An effective risk committee engages in robust inquiry into material or persistent breaches of risk appetite and risk limits, timeliness of remediation, and appropriateness of the audit plan. (SR 21-3/CA 21-1 Attachment)The OCC Heightened Standards require that the board or risk committee approve the risk governance framework, approve appointment or removal and annual compensation of the Chief Risk Executive(s), and ensure the CRE has unrestricted access to the board and its committees. (OCC Appendix D to 12 C.F.R. Part 30)Under 12 C.F.R. Section 252.33, the holding company CRO must report directly to both the risk committee and the CEO, and the company must ensure CRO compensation is ﾒconsistent with providing an objective assessment of the risks taken by the bank holding company.ﾓ (12 C.F.R. Section 252.33)B. Audit CommitteeSR 21-3 states that an effective audit committee meets directly with the chief audit executive, supports internal auditﾕs budget and staffing, and reviews the status of actions to remediate deficiencies identified by internal audit, external audit, and supervisors. (SR 21-3/CA 21-1 Attachment)The OCC Heightened Standards require the audit committee to approve all decisions regarding appointment, removal, and annual compensation of the Chief Audit Executive, and to review and approve internal auditﾕs charter and audit plans. The CAE must have unrestricted access to the boardﾕs audit committee. (OCC Appendix D to 12 C.F.R. Part 30)SEC Regulation S-K Item 407(d)(5) requires disclosure of whether at least one audit committee financial expert serves on the committee. (Reg S-K Rule 229.407)C. Compensation CommitteeThe 2010 Interagency Guidance on Sound Incentive Compensation Policies requires that incentive compensation arrangements be supported by strong corporate governance, including active and effective board oversight. Large banking organizations must have systematic policies for identifying and reviewing incentive compensation arrangements for all employees who can expose the organization to material risk. (OCC Bulletin 2010-24, Interagency Guidance on Sound Incentive Compensation Policies)SR 21-3 requires that an effective board oversee and evaluate performance management and compensation programs that encourage behaviors consistent with strategy, risk appetite, and safety and soundness. The board must approve clear performance objectives for the CEO, business line executives, CRO, and CAE. (SR 21-3/CA 21-1 Attachment)The 2024 re-proposed incentive compensation rule (FDIC, OCC, and FHFA) would require Level 1 and Level 2 institutions to establish a board compensation committee composed solely of non-executive directors, which would obtain input from risk and audit committees on the effectiveness of risk measures in incentive compensation. Management and an independent function would each be required to submit annual written assessments of the incentive compensation program to the compensation committee. Note: The Federal Reserve has not joined this proposal as of May 2026; it remains a proposed rule, not yet final. (FDIC FIL-21-2024, Incentive-Based Compensation Arrangements)D. Nominating/Governance CommitteeSR 21-3 requires that an effective board oversee succession plans for the CEO, and, depending on the firmﾕs complexity, the CRO, CAE, or other senior management officials. The guidance acknowledges this ﾒmay extend beyond requirements to which firms may be subject under other statutory and regulatory authorities, such as the NYSE requirement for formalized succession planning for the CEO only.ﾓ (SR 21-3/CA 21-1 Attachment)The OCC Heightened Standards require the board to appoint the CEO and appoint or approve the CAE and CRE(s), and to review and approve a written talent management program covering development, recruitment, and succession planning. (OCC Appendix D to 12 C.F.R. Part 30)SEC Regulation S-K Item 407(c) requires detailed disclosure of the nominating committeeﾕs nominee evaluation process, minimum qualifications, consideration of diversity, security holder recommendation procedures, and nominee source identification. (Reg S-K Rule 229.407; 68 FR 66992)VII. Capital Planning and Stress TestingThe Federal Reserveﾕs Regulation YY (12 C.F.R. Section 252.47) requires the board to review and approve the firmﾕs capital plan. SR 21-3 explicitly identifies the capital plan as a significant policy requiring board review and approval. (SR 21-3/CA 21-1 Attachment, citing 12 C.F.R. 225.8(e)(iii) and 12 C.F.R. 252.47(a))The OCC Heightened Standards require the risk governance framework to incorporate stress testing into capital planning processes and require the board to approve a risk appetite statement with quantitative limits addressing capital. (OCC Appendix D to 12 C.F.R. Part 30)The 2012 Interagency Supervisory Guidance on Stress Testing requires that boards have written stress testing policies, approved and annually reviewed, that direct and govern implementation of the stress testing framework. Boards should actively evaluate stress testing results and consider them for decisions relating to capital adequacy. (77 FR 29458, Supervisory Guidance on Stress Testing)VIII. Liquidity Risk Management12 C.F.R. Section 252.34(a) requires the board to approve the firmﾕs liquidity risk management policies. 12 C.F.R. Section 252.33(a)(1) assigns the risk committee responsibility for overseeing liquidity risk management as part of enterprise-wide risk management. (SR 21-3/CA 21-1 Attachment, citing 12 C.F.R. 252.34(a))The OCC Heightened Standards require the risk appetite statement to include quantitative limits addressing liquidity and require the strategic plan to contain a comprehensive assessment of liquidity risk. (OCC Appendix D to 12 C.F.R. Part 30)The interagency stress testing guidance (77 FR 29458) requires the board to consider stress testing results for decisions relating to liquidity adequacy, including review of the contingency funding plan. (77 FR 29458, Supervisory Guidance on Stress Testing)The interagency leveraged lending guidance (78 FR 17766) requires the board to approve the institutionﾕs risk appetite for leveraged lending activities with analysis of potential liquidity effects, and to receive at least quarterly summaries of management reports on leveraged exposures including liquidity stress test results. (77 FR 19417, Proposed Guidance on Leveraged Lending)IX. Resolution Planning12 U.S.C. Section 5365(d)(1) requires each Category I GSIB to develop and maintain a plan for rapid and orderly resolution in the event of material financial distress or failure. SR 21-3 explicitly identifies recovery and resolution plans as significant policies requiring board review and approval. (SR 21-3/CA 21-1 Attachment, citing 12 C.F.R. Part 243 and SR Letter 14-8)SR 12-17 states that resolution plans ﾒshould be utilized as an element of the firmﾕs strategic planning and address the complexity and interconnectivity of the firmﾕs operations.ﾓ (SR 12-17/CA 12-14)If the Board of Governors and the FDIC jointly determine a resolution plan is not credible, the company must resubmit with revisions. Failure to resubmit a compliant plan within two years can result in joint directives to divest assets or operations. (12 U.S.C. Section 5365)X. Recovery PlanningSR 14-8 provides guidance on recovery planning for the eight domestic LISCC bank holding companies. The board (or a designated committee) is responsible for overseeing the firmﾕs recovery planning process, including the firmﾕs ability to identify and implement recovery options and oversee managementﾕs remediation of identified weaknesses. A firm should review and update its recovery plan at least annually. Recovery planning should be integrated into corporate governance and operating processes and be part of business-as-usual activities. (SR 14-8, Consolidated Recovery Planning for Certain Large Domestic Bank Holding Companies)SR 14-1 established heightened supervisory expectations for recovery and resolution preparedness for the eight domestic GSIBs, requiring effective processes for managing collateral, understanding payment, clearing, and settlement obligations, analyzing funding sources, demonstrating MIS capabilities, and maintaining robust arrangements for continued provision of shared services. (SR 14-1, Heightened Supervisory Expectations for Recovery and Resolution Preparedness)XI. Operational ResilienceSR 12-17 requires each firm to ensure that critical operations are sufficiently resilient to be maintained, continued, and funded even in the event of failure or material financial or operational distress. (SR 12-17/CA 12-14)SR 21-3 defines operational strength and resilience as maintaining effective governance and controls to provide for continuity of the consolidated organization through a range of conditions. (SR 21-3/CA 21-1 Attachment)The OCC Heightened Standards require the risk governance framework to cover operational risk, including policies and processes for identifying, measuring, monitoring, and controlling operational risk concentrations. (OCC Appendix D to 12 C.F.R. Part 30)The 2023 Interagency Guidance on Third-Party Relationships states that the board has ultimate responsibility for providing oversight for third-party risk management and holding management accountable, and that a banking organizationﾕs use of third parties does not diminish its responsibility to operate in a safe and sound manner. (Interagency Guidance on Third-Party Relationships: Risk Management, June 2023)XII. Model Risk GovernanceThe 2011 Interagency Supervisory Guidance on Model Risk Management (SR 11-7/OCC Bulletin 2011-12) directed a bankﾕs ﾒboard or its delegatesﾓ to ﾒapprove model risk management policies and review them annuallyﾓ and stated that audit findings related to models should be ﾒreported to the board or its appropriately delegated agent.ﾓ (OCC Bulletin 2011-12, Supervisory Guidance on Model Risk Management)On April 17, 2026, the OCC, Federal Reserve, and FDIC issued revised interagency model risk management guidance (OCC Bulletin 2026-13/SR 26-2) that supersedes the 2011 guidance. The revised guidance explicitly states it ﾒdoes not set forth enforceable standards or prescriptive requirementsﾓ and that ﾒnon-compliance with this guidance will not result in supervisory criticism.ﾓ It removes the specific board-level expectations present in the 2011 guidance, replacing them with the general observation that model risk management benefits from ﾒclear roles and responsibilities with well-defined accountability.ﾓ (OCC Bulletin 2026-13, Model Risk Management: Revised Guidance)According to Sullivan & Cromwellﾕs analysis, the revised guidance affords banking organizations ﾒconsiderable discretion in designing their model risk management programsﾓ and may validate the continued use of previously adopted tailored frameworks. This is Sullivan & Cromwellﾕs interpretation of the guidance. (Sullivan & Cromwell, Federal Banking Agencies Issue Revised Guidance on Model Risk Management)However, binding board-level model risk obligations remain in force through the advanced approaches capital framework. 12 C.F.R. Section 217.122(i)(2) requires the board or a designated committee to at least annually review the effectiveness of, and approve, the institutionﾕs advanced systems. Section 217.122(i)(5) requires internal audit to report findings to the board or a committee thereof annually. Section 217.122(g)(1)(iii) requires operational risk information to be reported to the board or a designated committee. (12 C.F.R. Section 217.122)XIII. Third-Party Risk ManagementThe 2023 Interagency Guidance on Third-Party Relationships establishes that the board has ﾒultimate responsibility for providing oversight for third-party risk management and holding management accountable.ﾓ The board provides ﾒclear guidance regarding acceptable risk appetite, approves appropriate policies, and ensures that appropriate procedures and practices have been established.ﾓ The board or a designated committee should consider whether third-party relationships are managed consistently with strategic goals and risk appetite, whether there is appropriate periodic reporting, and whether management has remediated identified issues. As part of its oversight, the board should be aware of, and as appropriate approve, contracts involving higher-risk activities. (Interagency Guidance on Third-Party Relationships: Risk Management, June 2023)According to Debevoise & Plimptonﾕs analysis, revisions to the Federal Reserveﾕs vendor risk management guidance (SR 13-19) eliminated the expectation that the board approve vendor risk management policies, though senior management is expected to provide the board with sufficient information regarding risks arising from such relationships. This is a law firmﾕs interpretation of guidance revisions. (Debevoise & Plimpton, Federal Reserve Board Finalizes Board Effectiveness Guidance)XIV. Cybersecurity GovernanceBoard-level cybersecurity obligations arise from multiple overlapping frameworks that constitute one of the clearest examples of policy sludge in the GSIB governance regime.A. GLBA Information Security ProgramThe Interagency Guidelines Establishing Information Security Standards, issued pursuant to Section 501(b) of the Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act, require the board (or an appropriate committee) to: (1) approve the institutionﾕs written information security program; (2) oversee the development, implementation, and maintenance of the program, including assigning specific management responsibility and reviewing management reports; and (3) receive at least annual management reports on program status, risk assessments, risk management decisions, service provider arrangements, testing results, security breaches, and recommendations for changes.These identical obligations appear in four separately codified instruments:･ Appendix B to 12 C.F.R. Part 30 (OCC, national banks) (OCC Rules Part 30 Appendix B)･ Appendix D-2 to 12 C.F.R. Part 208 (Federal Reserve, state member banks) (FRS Rules Part 208 Appendix D-2)･ Appendix F to 12 C.F.R. Part 225 (Federal Reserve, bank holding companies) (FRS Rules Part 225 Appendix F)･ Appendix B to 12 C.F.R. Part 364 (FDIC) (FDIC Rules Part 364 Appendix B)The 2001 joint final rule confirmed these obligations are intended to be materially identical across charter types, but preserved the parallel codification structure. (66 FR 8616, Interagency Guidelines Establishing Standards for Safeguarding Customer Information)This is the clearest instance of structural redundancy in the GSIB board obligation framework. A director serving on both the holding company board and the national bank subsidiary board technically discharges four separately codified legal obligations each time she participates in the approval, oversight, or reporting review of the information security program.B. Incident NotificationThe Computer Security Incident Notification Rule (12 C.F.R. The Structural ProblemThe inventory developed in the preceding sections documents a board governance regime of extraordinary density. Category I GSIB boards are simultaneously subject to binding regulations from multiple agencies, operationally mandatory supervisory guidance from multiple agencies, interagency standards, domain-specific approval and reporting requirements, and qualitative supervisory expectations whose content is determined through examination rather than rulemaking.This framework did not emerge from a single, coherent regulatory design. Obligations accumulated piecemeal, from multiple agencies exercising overlapping jurisdiction, in response to successive crisis events, through a variety of legal instruments, without systematic mechanisms for removing or consolidating prior requirements as new ones were added. The result is a ratchet effect: obligations accumulate upward but are rarely removed.B. Categories of Identified Overlap1. Cross-Agency Duplication: Federal Reserve / OCC.
