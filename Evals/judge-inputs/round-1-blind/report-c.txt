This document inventories, at the level of the individual citation, the legally operative obligations that current federal banking regulation and supervisory guidance place on the boards of directors of the eight U.S. global systemically important banking organizations (ŇGSIBsÓ) Ń the firms the Federal Reserve treats as ŇCategory IÓ under its tailoring framework Ń and on the boards of their insured depository institution (ŇIDIÓ) subsidiaries. It is built to serve two audiences at once: directors and their counsel, who need to know precisely what each board and each board committee must do and under which authority; and policymakers, who need a defensible map of where those duties duplicate, overlap, or impose burden disproportionate to their marginal supervisory value.Scope of the population. Category I status is not an asset threshold. A firm is a Category I GSIB only if it is identified as a U.S. GSIB under 12 CFR 217.402 Ń that is, its method-1 systemic-indicator score equals or exceeds 130 basis points. As of the Financial Stability BoardŐs November 2025 list, the eight U.S. GSIBs are JPMorgan Chase, Bank of America, Citigroup, Wells Fargo, Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, Bank of New York Mellon, and State Street. Each sits atop a corporate group containing one or more large IDIs, and the obligations catalogued here therefore operate at two distinct legal levels that this document keeps rigorously separate.Principal structural finding. Board obligations divide cleanly along a holding-company / insured-bank seam. The enhanced-prudential-standards regime built on Dodd-Frank ¤165 Ń the risk committee, the capital plan, the liquidity risk tolerance, the company-run stress tests, the Title I resolution plan Ń attaches to the top-tier publicly traded holding-company board. The governance regime built on the Federal Deposit Insurance Act Ń the safety-and-soundness standards, the OCCŐs heightened standards, the FDICIA audit committee, the BSA program, the IDI resolution plan Ń attaches at the insured-bank level, though several of these expressly permit a holding-company board or committee to serve the subsidiary function. A GSIB board structure must satisfy both regimes simultaneously, frequently through interlocking parent and subsidiary boards and committees.The duplication problem in one sentence. The same underlying board duty Ń approve a written program, oversee its implementation, receive periodic reporting, exercise credible challenge of management Ń recurs across at least a dozen separate authorities issued by four agencies, each with its own threshold, its own approval cadence, its own independence test, and its own documentation expectation, so that a single GSIB board faces multiple differently-worded versions of substantially the same obligation.Currency caveat (important). The 2025Đ2026 period saw unusually heavy movement in this area. The interagency model risk management guidance (former SR 11-7) was rescinded and replaced on April 17, 2026; the OCCŐs recovery-planning guidelines (12 CFR part 30, appendix E) are rescinded effective May 1, 2026; the FDICŐs 2023 corporate-governance proposal was withdrawn on March 3, 2025; the FFIEC Cybersecurity Assessment Tool was sunset on August 31, 2025; and an interagency rule restricting the supervisory use of Ňreputation riskÓ took effect in April 2026. Each is flagged in place and consolidated in the Currency and Transition Notes appendix.How this document is organizedPart A Ń Foundations. The precise authority Ń the CFR section, appendix, and paragraph; the supervisory letter or bulletin number; the Federal Register citation; or the statutory section.Ą Board-level duty. The nature of what the board must do Ń approve, oversee, review, receive, ensure, hold accountable, or some combination.Ą Allocation. Whether the duty rests on the full board, may be delegated to a designated board committee, or is assigned to a specific named committee (risk, audit, compensation, nominating/governance).Ą Director distinction. Any requirement that turns on inside versus outside directors, or independent versus non-independent directors.Ą Frequency. The required cadence Ń annual, semiannual, quarterly, periodic, or event-driven.Ą Entity level. Whether the duty attaches at the top-tier bank holding company (ŇBHCÓ), the insured depository institution (ŇIDIÓ), the public issuer, or more than one.A note on ŇshallÓ versus Ňshould.Ó Many of the most consequential board duties live in interagency guidelines and supervisory guidance that use Ňshould.Ó For the safety-and-soundness guidelines issued under section 39 of the FDI Act (12 U.S.C. 1831p-1), ŇshouldÓ language is nonetheless enforceable: a failure to meet a standard can trigger a mandatory compliance plan and, ultimately, an enforceable order. By contrast, the April 2026 model risk guidance expressly disclaims enforceability. The entries note this distinction where it matters.Sources and verificationThe authorities catalogued here were drawn from the Electronic Code of Federal Regulations, the Federal Register, and the official websites of the Federal Reserve, the OCC, the FDIC, the CFPB, the FFIEC, and FinCEN, supplemented by the United States Code. This inventory addresses board-level duties. It does not catalogue the far larger body of management-level and firm-level obligations, except where naming them is necessary to locate the boardŐs oversight role. Purely quantitative requirements that carry no board-governance duty Ń the stress capital buffer mechanics of 12 CFR 217.11, the LCR and NSFR ratios of 12 CFR part 249, and the 36-hour computer-security incident notice Ń are identified as such and then set aside.Chapter 1 Ń The Category I Population, the Statutory Architecture, and the Allocation of Board Duties1.1  What makes a firm ŇCategory IÓCategory I is the apex tier of the Federal ReserveŐs tailoring framework, codified at 12 CFR 252.5 (Regulation YY), which the Board added through the 2019 tailoring rule (84 FR 59032, Nov. 1, 2019). The framework sorts large banking organizations into four categories. Critically, Category I is not defined by an asset threshold. Section 252.5(b) provides that a banking organization is in Category I if and only if it Ňis a global systemically important BHC,Ó which in turn means it Ňis identified as a global systemically important BHC pursuant to 12 CFR 217.402.ÓA banking organization is a global systemically important BHC if it is identified as a global systemically important BHC pursuant to 12 CFR 217.402.  Ń 12 CFR 252.5(b)Identification under 12 CFR 217.402 (Regulation Q, Subpart H) turns on a single quantitative test: a bank holding company is a U.S. GSIB if its Ňmethod 1 score,Ó calculated under 12 CFR 217.404, Ňequals or exceeds 130 basis points,Ó measured annually as of December 31. The method-1 score aggregates twelve systemic indicators across five categories Ń size, interconnectedness, substitutability, complexity, and cross-jurisdictional activity. (The separate method-2 score under 12 CFR 217.405Đ.406, which incorporates a short-term wholesale funding measure, is used to size the capital surcharge, not to identify GSIB status.) The consequence for this inventory is that Category I status, and the heaviest board obligations attached to it, follows from systemic footprint rather than from any single balance-sheet number.By contrast, Categories II through IV are defined by asset and risk thresholds in 12 CFR 252.5(c)Đ(e): Category II by $700 billion in assets or $75 billion in cross-jurisdictional activity; Category III by $250 billion in assets or $100 billion in assets coupled with $75 billion in nonbank assets, weighted short-term wholesale funding, or off-balance-sheet exposure; and Category IV as the residual $100-billion-and-above tier. Several obligations in this document apply across multiple categories; where a duty is specific to Category I, or is calibrated more stringently for a GSIB, the entry says so.1.2  The eight Category I firmsAs of the Financial Stability BoardŐs 2025 list (published November 27, 2025, on end-2024 data), the eight U.S. GSIBs Ń and therefore the Category I population Ń are JPMorgan Chase, Bank of America, Citigroup, Wells Fargo, Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, Bank of New York Mellon, and State Street. The FSB list and the Federal ReserveŐs 12 CFR 217.402 identification are legally distinct mechanisms (the former international, the latter domestic), but for these eight firms they coincide. The operative U.S. citation for any given firmŐs status is its own annual method-1 determination under ¤ 217.402, not the FSB list.1.3  The statutory architecture in briefBoard obligations on these firms rest on two statutory pillars. Understanding the pillars explains why the duties divide as they do.Dodd-Frank ¤165 (12 U.S.C. 5365) Ń enhanced prudential standards.  Section 165 directs the Federal Reserve to impose heightened capital, liquidity, risk-management, resolution-planning, and stress-testing standards on large bank holding companies, and to tailor those standards by category (¤165(a)(2)). Section 165(h) commands a board-level risk committee. Section 165(i) commands stress testing. Section 165(d) commands resolution plans. Section 166 (12 U.S.C. 5366) directs an early-remediation regime, which remains largely unimplemented as a standalone rule and is flagged here only so the reader does not search for a CFR section that does not exist.FDI Act ¤39 and ¤36 Ń bank-level governance.  Section 39 (12 U.S.C. 1831p-1) directs the banking agencies to prescribe safety-and-soundness standards for insured depository institutions, including internal controls, internal audit, and compensation standards; these are implemented as interagency guidelines and, for the largest national banks, as the OCCŐs heightened standards. Section 36 (12 U.S.C. 1831m) commands an independent, outside-director audit committee at the insured-bank level and is implemented by 12 CFR part 363.1.4  The allocation principle: holding-company board versus insured-bank boardThe single most important structural fact in this inventory is that board obligations attach at two different legal levels, and the level depends on the statutory pillar. The enhanced-prudential-standards duties built on ¤165 attach to the top-tier, publicly traded bank holding company board. The FDI-Act governance duties attach at the insured depository institution level Ń though several of them expressly permit a holding-company board or committee to discharge the subsidiary function. A GSIBŐs board architecture must therefore satisfy both regimes at once.MandateAuthorityLegal levelMay a holding-company board / committee serve?Enterprise risk committee + CRODFA ¤165(h); 12 CFR 252.33Top-tier BHC board committeeIt is the BHC boardŐs own committee; no IDI analog in Reg YYCapital plan; liquidity risk tolerance; company-run stress tests; Title I resolution planDFA ¤165; 12 CFR 225.8, 252.34, 252.56, 243.4Top-tier BHC boardDuty sits at the BHC; not imposed on the IDI boardSafety-and-soundness standards; OCC heightened standardsFDIA ¤39; 12 CFR pt 30 App. A & D, pt 364 App. A, pt 208 App. D-1IDI boardStandards run to the IDI; OCC App. D imposes duties on the covered bankŐs own boardAudit committeeFDIA ¤36; 12 CFR 363.5IDI board committeeYes Ń ¤1831m(i) and ¤363.5(b) permit the holding-company audit committee to serve, subject to the large-customer exclusionIDI resolution plan12 CFR 360.10IDI (covered insured depository institution) boardNo; the CIDI board must approve (insured branches may delegate)Two refinements matter. It is written for the policymaker engaged in reform, and it is deliberately specific: each pattern is tied to the citations catalogued above so that a drafter can act on it. Identifying overlap is not the same as recommending repeal; in several places the redundancy is benign or even protective. The aim is to surface the candidates and characterize them, not to prejudge the policy outcome.15.1  Pattern one Ń the recurring Ňapprove a program, oversee it, receive periodic reportingÓ templateThe most pervasive form of duplication is structural rather than substantive. Across at least eight separate authorities, the board is told to do the same three things in respect of a different risk: approve a written program or policy, oversee managementŐs implementation, and receive periodic (usually annual) reporting. The template recurs in:Ą the information-security guidelines (¤III.A approve, ¤III.F annual report);Ą the BSA program rules (12 CFR 21.21(c)(1), 208.63(b)(1), 326.8(b)(1) Ń written, approved, minuted);Ą the third-party guidance (88 FR 37935 Ń approve policies, oversee, periodic reporting);Ą the liquidity rules (12 CFR 252.34(a)(2) and SR 10-6 Ń approve strategies/policies, review at least annually);Ą the capital-plan rule and SR 15-18 (approve plan and policy, quarterly briefings);Ą the heightened standards (approve risk-governance framework and risk-appetite statement, monitor quarterly);Ą the operational-resilience sound practices (approve and periodically review tolerance for disruption);Ą the consumer CMS expectations (adopt the program, oversee, receive audit reporting).Why it matters. A Category I board confronts the same governance verb Ń approve, oversee, receive Ń eight or more times a year on eight or more separate programs, each documented separately to satisfy a separate examiner. The marginal protection from requiring a distinct, separately minuted board approval of each individual program (rather than, say, a consolidated risk-governance approval coupled with risk-specific management certifications) is plausibly small, while the agenda load, documentation cost, and dilution of board attention are real. This is the single largest target for consolidation: a unified, principles-based board-oversight standard could subsume most of the eight without lowering the substantive expectation.15.2  Pattern two Ń non-uniform independence definitions for the same directorsAs Chapter 3 documents, a GSIB lead bank and its parent are subject to at least six distinct independence tests Ń the heightened-standards two-independent-director rule, the Reg YY risk-committee-chair test, the FDICIA outside-director definition, the SOX ¤301 audit-committee test, the DFA ¤952 compensation-committee test, and the exchange listing standards. They differ in lookback period (one year for FDICIA, three years for Reg YY and the heightened standards), in the family-relationship and affiliation tests, and in whether they incorporate an exchange standard. The reform opportunity is harmonization: a single cross-referenced definition of director independence for prudential purposes would eliminate the need for governance staff to maintain parallel independence determinations for the same individuals, with no loss of independence in substance.15.3  Pattern three Ń parallel approvals at the parent and bank levelsSeveral duties require formal board action at both the holding-company and the insured-bank level on overlapping subject matter:SubjectBHC-level board dutyIDI-level board dutyOverlap characterResolution planningTitle I plan approval, 12 CFR 243.4(f)IDI plan approval, 12 CFR 360.10(c)(5)Two minuted approvals on overlapping content, two cadences, two rulesLiquidity governance252.34(a) tolerance & strategiesSR 10-6 board ownership & annual reviewSubstantially the same board duty stated twice at two levelsRecovery planningSR 14-8 oversightpt 30 app. E approval (until 5/1/2026)Duplicative until the OCC rescission; then parent-onlyRisk governance / appetite252.33 risk committee; SR 21-3pt 30 app. D framework & appetiteParent risk committee and bank risk-governance framework cover overlapping groundInformation securitypt 225 app. F program approvalpt 30 app. B / 364 app. B program approvalSame program, approved at two levelsCharacterization. The board-approval-and-annual-review duty under former SR 11-7 was removed entirely on April 17, 2026; the replacement guidance imposes no express board duty and disclaims enforceability. Firms that built board-level model-risk approval calendars now hold a duty the agencies no longer require.Ą Recovery planning. The OCCŐs codified bank-board approval duty (12 CFR part 30, app. E) is rescinded effective May 1, 2026, leaving the holding-company SR 14-8 oversight duty as the principal hook.Ą Corporate governance. The FDICŐs 2023 proposed Appendix C to part 364 Ń which would have layered a detailed board-and-risk-governance regime on $10B+ institutions Ń was withdrawn on March 3, 2025, so it imposes nothing; reformers should not treat it as latent law.Ą Incentive compensation. Dodd-Frank ¤956 has never been finalized across three rounds of proposals (2011, 2016, 2024); 12 CFR part 236 is reserved. The only operative standard is the 2010 guidance.Ą Cyber assessment. The FFIEC CAT was sunset August 31, 2025; board expectations built around it are now untethered from any FFIEC instrument.Implication. Roughly a quarter of the board duties a 2024-vintage compliance map would have listed in these five areas have been removed, softened, or were never in force. A current inventory must be dated, and a reform effort can claim some of this simplification as already achieved Ń while noting that frequent change is itself a form of burden.15.5  Pattern five Ń ŇshallÓ versus Ňshould,Ó and the enforceability gradientBoard duties in this area sit on a gradient from hard rule (the capital-plan, resolution-plan, BSA, and audit-committee requirements, which are codified ŇshallÓ obligations) through enforceable guidelines (the ¤39 safety-and-soundness and heightened standards, framed in ŇshouldÓ but backed by the compliance-plan and order regime) to non-enforceable guidance (the April 2026 model-risk guidance, which disclaims enforceability). For directors, the practical expectation often does not track the legal form: examiners may press ŇshouldÓ expectations as firmly as ŇshallÓ rules. For reformers, the gradient is an opportunity to be explicit about which expectations are truly binding, reducing the defensive over-compliance that ambiguity invites.15.6  A short menu of consolidation candidatesSynthesizing the patterns, the strongest candidates for burden reduction without loss of substantive protection are:1. A unified board risk-governance approval. Replace the eight separate program-approval-and-reporting duties (15.1) with one consolidated, principles-based board-oversight standard plus risk-specific management certifications.2. A single prudential definition of director independence. Harmonize the six independence tests (15.2) through one cross-referenced standard.3. Rationalized parent/bank approvals. Permit a single board approval to satisfy both levels where the supervisory interests are not genuinely distinct (15.3), preserving dual approval only for resolution planning and similar cases.4. Codification clarity. State plainly which guidance is enforceable and retire instruments overtaken by events (15.4Đ15.5).A balancing note. The case against aggressive consolidation deserves a fair statement. Separate, risk-specific board approvals create a documented record that focuses board attention on each material risk in turn and gives each agency a clean supervisory hook in its own domain; collapsing them risks a return to generalized, low-information board sign-offs of the kind that pre-crisis governance failures exposed. The dual parent/bank structure reflects the reality that an insured bank and its holding company can fail for different reasons and must be governed as distinct legal persons. The reform question is therefore not whether board oversight should be reduced, but whether the same level of substantive oversight can be achieved through fewer, better-integrated obligations Ń and that is an empirical and prudential judgment for the agencies, informed by the map this document provides.Appendix A Ń Master Obligation MatrixA single-table view of the board-level obligations catalogued in this document.
