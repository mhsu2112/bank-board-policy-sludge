Board-Level Obligations Applicable to Category I U.S. GSIB Bank Holding Companies and Their Insured Depository Institution SubsidiariesComprehensive Citation-Level Working Draft for Directors, Counsel, and Regulatory Reform PolicymakersCurrent through May 29, 2026
Important Note on UseThis document is a working policy-reform inventory, not legal advice. It is designed to identify board-level obligations that arise from federal banking regulations, enforceable safety-and-soundness guidelines, and supervisory guidance applicable to Category I U.S. global systemically important bank holding companies and their insured depository institution subsidiaries. It should be validated by counsel against the current Code of Federal Regulations, agency manuals, supervisory letters, and any institution-specific orders, commitments, or supervisory findings before use in board materials or advocacy.The document uses ŇobligationÓ in a functional sense. It distinguishes binding regulations, enforceable guidelines, supervisory guidance, and examination expectations. That distinction matters. Regulations and enforceable guidelines can create direct legal obligations. Supervisory guidance and examination manuals generally do not create binding law, but they often shape examiner expectations, board reporting packages, and governance burdens in practice.Executive Summary* Category I GSIB boards face a dense board-governance stack built from at least five overlapping layers: Federal Reserve enhanced prudential standards for the parent bank holding company; IDI-level safety-and-soundness and charter-specific regimes; committee-specific mandates; program-specific approval and reporting obligations; and supervisory guidance overlays in capital, liquidity, resolution, model risk, third-party risk, cybersecurity, incentive compensation, BSA/AML, and consumer compliance.* The most prescriptive board requirements are not evenly distributed. The clearest hard-law requirements include: maintenance of a BHC board risk committee under Regulation YY; board or committee approval of liquidity risk tolerance, liquidity policies, and contingency funding plan elements; board approval and minute notation for Title I resolution plans; board approval and minute notation for IDI resolution submissions; audit committee independence under FDICIA part 363; board approval of BSA/AML compliance programs; and board or committee approval and oversight of information-security programs under the interagency GLBA safeguards guidelines.* The largest source of duplication for Category I firms is the mismatch between enterprise-level consolidated supervision and legal-entity-level IDI governance. A GSIB parent board and a major bank subsidiary board may be asked to approve or review similar risk, liquidity, resolution, model, third-party, cyber, and compliance materials even when the underlying framework is enterprise-wide.* A second major source of burden is the conversion of oversight into document approval. In many domains, the board or board committee is not merely expected to oversee management but to approve written programs, policies, plans, risk tolerances, contingency funding plans, BSA/AML programs, information-security programs, and resolution submissions.* A third source of burden is inconsistent terminology for director status. ŇIndependent director,Ó Ňoutside director,Ó Ňindependent of management,Ó and exchange-listing independence are not identical concepts. Regulations impose different tests for BHC risk committees, IDI audit committees, and OCC heightened-standards boards.* The most promising reform direction is not to weaken board oversight. It is to distinguish nondelegable board duties from management tasks, harmonize approval cycles, allow clear reliance by subsidiary boards on parent-level committee work where legally appropriate, and convert redundant plan approvals into risk-based certifications or exception reporting.Methodology and Source HierarchyThe inventory was built from four source categories. First are binding regulations in Title 12 of the Code of Federal Regulations, including Federal Reserve Regulation YY, Regulation Y, Regulation Q, FDIC part 363, FDIC part 360, OCC part 30, OCC part 21, FDIC part 326, Federal Reserve part 208, and the interagency information-security guidelines. Second are enforceable safety-and-soundness guidelines issued under section 39 of the Federal Deposit Insurance Act, including OCC appendix D heightened standards and the interagency safety-and-soundness and information-security guidelines. Third are supervisory letters and interagency guidance, including SR 12-17, SR 14-8, SR 15-18, SR 21-3, SR 23-4, and SR 26-2. Fourth are examination manuals and procedures from the CFPB and other agencies, which are not binding law but frequently drive board-level documentation and reporting practices.The inventory excludes proposed rules, exchange-listing rules, SEC proxy rules, state corporate-law fiduciary duties, private contractual obligations, confidential supervisory findings, and firm-specific enforcement actions. The OCC recovery-planning guidelines formerly located at 12 CFR part 30, appendix E are treated as rescinded and are not counted as current in-force obligations. Proposed Dodd-Frank Act section 956 incentive-compensation rules are not counted as current obligations because they have not been finalized.SourceLegal statusBoard-relevant contentFederal Reserve Regulation YY, 12 CFR part 252, subpart DBinding regulationBHC enhanced prudential standards, risk committee, liquidity risk management, liquidity stress testingFederal Reserve Regulation Y, 12 CFR 225.8Binding regulationCapital plan and stress capital buffer processFederal Reserve Regulation Q, 12 CFR part 217Binding regulationCapital adequacy and advanced approaches systems governanceFederal Reserve / FDIC Resolution Plan Rule, 12 CFR part 243 and 12 CFR part 381Binding regulationTitle I resolution planning for covered companiesFDIC 12 CFR 360.10Binding regulationCovered insured depository institution resolution planningFDIC 12 CFR part 363Binding regulationAnnual independent audit and audit committee requirementsOCC 12 CFR part 30, appendices A, B, and DEnforceable safety-and-soundness guidelinesSafety and soundness, information security, OCC heightened risk governanceOCC 12 CFR 21.21; Federal banking law does not impose a single integrated governance architecture. Instead, it layers BHC-level obligations, IDI-level obligations, and program-specific committee requirements. This creates overlapping accountability lines and a high volume of board packages.The inventory below treats the legally obligated actor as precisely as possible. In some rules the obligation is on the Ňbank holding company,Ó but the rule specifies a board or committee action. In others, the obligation is on the institution, but examiners evaluate the boardŐs oversight as a governance control. In still others, the obligation is on management to report to the board or committee. Parent-subsidiary duplicationCategory I firms are supervised both as consolidated BHCs and as IDI legal entities. The parent board oversees the enterprise risk framework, capital, liquidity, recovery/resolution, cyber, third-party, model, and consumer-compliance risks. Major IDI boards separately approve or oversee BSA programs, information-security programs, FDICIA audit matters, IDI resolution plans, and, for OCC covered banks, risk-governance frameworks and risk appetite. Where the enterprise operates through centralized risk and control functions, the same subject matter may move through multiple boards.2. Board approval proliferationThe inventory shows multiple mandatory approval points: liquidity risk tolerance, liquidity policies, contingency funding plan, Title I resolution plan, CIDI resolution plan, advanced approaches implementation plan, BSA/AML program, information-security program, and audit committee structures. In addition, supervisory guidance often leads boards to approve or review policies even where not legally required.3. Committee overlapRisk committees, audit committees, compensation committees, technology/cyber committees, and compliance committees may all receive partially overlapping materials. Model risk can appear in capital, stress testing, fair lending, AML, fraud, and AI reports. Third-party risk can appear in cyber, consumer compliance, operational resilience, and affiliate-management reports.4. Ambiguous board-management boundarySeveral sources try to distinguish oversight from management execution, but many obligations are written as program-approval requirements. Boards are therefore incentivized to review detailed documents to create an evidentiary record, even when the details are operational rather than strategic.5. Inconsistent independence vocabularyRisk committee independence, audit committee outside-director status, OCC independent-director expectations, and general independent judgment standards are not harmonized. This creates legal interpretive friction and complicates board composition planning.6. Guidance-driven hardeningSupervisory guidance and examination procedures do not impose binding requirements, but they harden into de facto board obligations when examiners expect board minutes, packages, committee charters, and periodic reporting to evidence oversight.Chapter 15 Ń Reform TaxonomyReform leverDescriptionMost relevant domainsDe-duplicate approvalsPermit one enterprise Board accountability is essential for large-bank governance; unnecessary duplication arises when the same risk is approved or reviewed repeatedly without adding incremental challenge.Principle 2: Regulate the board-management boundary. Board duties should be framed as direction, oversight, challenge, escalation, and accountability. Management duties should be framed as design, execution, monitoring, and remediation.Principle 3: Make legal-entity accountability compatible with enterprise risk management. GSIBs operate enterprise control functions, but IDI boards retain legal obligations. Reform should allow parent/subsidiary reliance with clear annexes and minutes.Principle 4: Require board attention to material changes. Static annual approvals should be replaced where possible by annual framework approval plus event-driven material-change approval and exception reporting.Principle 5: Harmonize vocabulary. Different agencies should use common definitions for board, board committee, appropriate committee, independent director, outside director, independent of management, and risk expert.Principle 6: Treat guidance as guidance. Agencies should not use examination expectations to convert nonbinding guidance into de facto rules requiring highly specific board artifacts.Appendix H Ń Source-by-Source Implementation NotesRegulation YY risk committee implementation noteFor Category I firms, the board risk committee should be treated as the central enterprise-risk oversight body. The rule expressly requires full board approval or minute notation. Reform requires regulatory amendment or express agency interpretation.* Code B: Board or committee approval. The rule allows approval by a board committee or appropriate committee. Reform can focus on committee allocation and consolidated packages.* Code C: Board oversight expectation. The source expects the board to oversee management or a framework, but does not require detailed document approval. Reform can clarify evidence expectations.* Code D: Management duty with board reporting. The operational obligation belongs to management, but management must report to the board or committee. Reform can consolidate reporting.* Code E: Examination evidence expectation. The source is an examination manual or procedure that prompts examiners to review board materials. Reform can focus on examiner training and guidance.* Code F: Cross-functional control obligation. The same risk appears in several programs, such as model risk in capital, AML, fair lending, and AI. Reform can consolidate dashboards and issue tracking.* Code G: Legal-entity duplication. Parent and subsidiary boards review the same enterprise framework. Reform can permit reliance with legal-entity annexes.* Code H: Terminology conflict. Different sources use different independence or committee terms. Reform can harmonize definitions.
