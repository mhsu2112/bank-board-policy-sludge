## 1. Scope and key assumptions

- **Jurisdiction**: U.S. federal law and regulation.
- **Firms in scope**: Category I **global systemically important bank holding companies** (“G‑SIB BHCs”) under the Federal Reserve’s tailoring framework (currently JPMorgan Chase, Bank of America, Citigroup, Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, Wells Fargo).[Category I is defined in 12 CFR part 217 (Reg Q) and 12 CFR part 252 (Reg YY) tailoring rules; these BHCs also appear on FSB’s G‑SIB list.[3][4][8]]
- **Entities**: The **top‑tier BHC** and its **insured depository institution** (“IDI”) subsidiaries (national banks, FSBs, FDIC‑insured state banks).
- **Agencies**: Federal Reserve (FRB), OCC, FDIC, CFPB, plus interagency standards (e.g., safety and soundness guidelines, model risk guidance).

“Obligations” here means:
- Explicit board or board‑committee duties stated in regulation, rule, or formal guidance; and
- Supervisory expectations that, in practice, function as required board behaviors for safety and soundness.

## 3. Cross‑cutting board obligations for large BHCs and IDIs

### 3.1 Core fiduciary and prudential duties (all bank/holding company boards)

- The FRB’s **Commercial Bank Examination Manual, Section 5000.1 “Duties and Responsibilities of Directors”** identifies the core responsibilities of a bank’s board:[2]
  - Ensure the bank is **sufficiently capitalized**; maintain capital adequacy policies and plans.[2]
  - Establish and approve **policies** for major activities (investments, loans, ALM, asset quality, internal controls).[2]
  - **Select and supervise competent management**, including appointing senior officers and ensuring their performance is evaluated.[2][1]
  - Determine compliance with **laws and regulations** and address conflicts of interest.[2]
  - Maintain a “complete understanding of the bank’s condition,” actively participate in board/committee meetings, and exercise independent judgment.[1][2]

- These duties fall on the **full board**, but can be executed via committees (audit, risk, etc.)—without delegating ultimate responsibility.[2]

### 3.2 Heightened standards for large/complex institutions

- **OCC Heightened Standards (12 CFR part 30, Appendix D)** impose explicit board duties for large national banks and FSBs:
  - The board must **actively oversee management** in implementing the bank’s risk governance framework and risk appetite statement.[Appendix D § II–III]
  - The board must provide “credible challenge” to management and hold management accountable.[Appendix D § III]
  - The board must **approve the risk appetite statement** and oversee adherence to it.[Appendix D § II]
  - The board must ensure the independent risk management and internal audit functions have sufficient stature and resources and report regularly to the board/risk committee.[Appendix D § II–IV]

- These standards apply to the **full board**, but also require the establishment of a **board‑level risk committee** and specify its responsibilities and composition (including independent directors).

## 4. Domain‑specific obligations, with citations and allocation

Below I map the principal domains you listed, identify key sources, and give representative board‑level obligations. For a 40–60 page memo, each bullet would be expanded, parsed by clause, and tied to internal policy references and committee charters.

### 4.1 Capital planning and stress testing

**Key sources**

- **Regulation YY** – 12 CFR part 252:
  - Capital plan rule (subpart E for BHCs; subpart F for IHCs) – FRB capital plan requirements for large BHCs.
  - Supervisory and company‑run stress testing requirements (now largely embedded in the capital plan process).
- **SR 15‑18 / SR 15‑19** – Capital planning for large and non‑complex firms.
- **Regulation Q** – GSIB surcharge and TLAC requirements (capital adequacy context).[8]
- FRB CBEM § 5000.1 (board oversight of capital).[2]

**Representative board obligations**

- **Approve the capital plan**:
  - A large BHC’s **board of directors or a designated board committee must review and approve the capital plan** before submission to the FRB; the board must ensure the plan is appropriate for the firm’s risk profile and capital adequacy needs.[The capital plan rule in Reg YY requires board approval of the capital plan; FRB capital planning guidance (SR 15‑18) further specifies the board’s responsibilities in overseeing capital adequacy.]
  - Duty of the **full board or risk/finance committee**, depending on internal allocation.

- **Oversee capital adequacy and stress testing framework**:
  - The board must have a **comprehensive understanding of the firm’s material risks** and how they are captured in capital adequacy processes, including stress tests.[2]
  - The board (and/or risk committee) must **review and approve key assumptions, scenarios, and methodologies** used in capital planning, or at least ensure they are subject to robust independent review (management and internal audit).[SR 15‑18]

- **GSIB surcharge and TLAC**:
  - The board must ensure the firm maintains regulatory capital, GSIB surcharge capital buffer, and TLAC/LTD amounts required under Regulation Q and TLAC rules.[8][5]
  - The risk/finance committee typically monitors compliance, but the **full board** retains ultimate responsibility.

**Director distinctions**

- Capital plan oversight is generally a **full‑board** obligation; however, supervisory expectations stress the role of the **independent risk committee** and **independent audit committee** in challenging management assumptions.

---

### 4.2 Liquidity risk management

**Key sources**

- **Regulation YY liquidity standards** for large BHCs (internal liquidity stress testing, liquidity buffers).
- **LCR and NSFR rules** (12 CFR parts 249 and related provisions).
- **OCC Heightened Standards** (for national banks) require a board‑approved risk appetite statement and risk governance framework incorporating liquidity risk.[Appendix D § II]
- FRB and OCC guidance on liquidity risk management.

**Representative board obligations**

- **Approve liquidity risk tolerance and governance**:
  - The board must **approve the firm’s liquidity risk tolerance** and ensure that management implements appropriate liquidity risk management practices consistent with that tolerance.[OCC Appendix D; FRB liquidity guidance]
  - The board or risk committee must periodically **review reports on liquidity positions, stress test results, and contingency funding plans**.

- **Oversee internal liquidity stress testing (ILST)**:
  - For large BHCs, Reg YY’s ILST requirements imply that the **board/risk committee must oversee the design and use of ILST results** in day‑to‑day and contingency planning.

- **Liquidity buffer and metrics**:
  - The board must ensure the institution maintains adequate **HQLA** (high quality liquid assets) and complies with LCR/NSFR requirements; the risk/ALCO committee typically monitors, but board has ultimate responsibility.

**Committee allocation**

- Typically, **Risk Committee** (board‑level) is the focal point; Audit Committee might review liquidity risk in the context of financial reporting and internal controls.

---

### 4.3 Resolution planning (living wills) and recovery planning

**Key sources**

- **Dodd‑Frank § 165(d)** and implementing rules (FRB/FDIC joint rule for resolution plans).
- **TLAC/LTD rule** for GSIB BHCs (12 CFR part 252 subpart G; Reg Q TLAC requirements).[5][8]
- FRB and FDIC guidance on resolution planning for large firms.
- FSB “Key Attributes of Effective Resolution Regimes” and TLAC term sheet, as imported into U.S. rules.[6]

**Representative board obligations**

- **Approve resolution plan content and governance**:
  - The **BHC board** is expected to **approve the firm’s resolution plan** and ensure that governance arrangements, legal entity structures, and operational capabilities support an orderly resolution under the preferred strategy (e.g., SPOE).[FDIC/Fed resolution planning guidance; TLAC/LTD rule design around SPOE.[5]]

- **Ensure resolvability capabilities**:
  - Board must oversee management’s efforts to maintain adequate **separability, clean holding company structure, TLAC debt, shared services arrangements, and financial resource location** that support resolution.
  - Board or risk committee should receive **regular reports** on resolvability assessments and remediation of identified obstacles.[3][4][6]

- **Recovery planning** (where applicable):
  - For large IDIs, FDIC has encouraged recovery planning; boards should **review and approve recovery plans**, ensuring credible options to restore financial strength under stress.

**Committee allocation**

- Often housed in **Risk Committee** (or a dedicated “Resolution & Recovery Committee” in some GSIBs); Audit Committee focuses on valuation and reporting assumptions relevant to resolution.

### 4.4 Operational resilience and operational risk governance

**Key sources**

- **OCC Heightened Standards**: require a risk governance framework covering operational risk and internal controls, with board oversight.[Appendix D]
- **Interagency safety and soundness guidelines**: internal controls, information systems, and internal audit.[OCC part 30 App. A/B; FDIC part 364 App. A/B]
- FRB/OCC/FDIC guidance on operational risk management and operational resilience (including IT, business continuity, and cyber).

**Representative board obligations**

- **Approve and oversee risk governance framework**:
  - Under OCC Heightened Standards, the board must **approve and periodically review the risk governance framework** that covers operational risk, including internal control systems and operational risk appetite.[Appendix D]
  - Board must ensure **front line units, independent risk management, and internal audit** are defined and functioning.

- **Oversight of internal controls and audit**:
  - Interagency safety and soundness guidelines require the **board and senior management to ensure that the institution has an adequate system of internal controls**; the **board or audit committee** must **approve and review the internal audit function** and receive audit reports, including on operational risk.[OCC part 30 App. A; FDIC part 364 App. A]

- **Business continuity and operational resilience**:
  - Board must oversee management’s **business continuity planning**, including testing and crisis management protocols; this obligation is often articulated in OCC/FRB handbooks and guidance on operational resilience.

**Committee allocation**

- **Audit Committee**: internal control systems and internal audit oversight.
- **Risk Committee**: operational risk framework, risk appetite, and risk dashboards.

### 4.5 Model risk governance

**Key source**

- **FRB SR 11‑7 – Guidance on Model Risk Management**, adopted by OCC and FDIC via parallel guidance, constitutes the de facto U.S. standard for model risk governance in large banks.

**Representative board obligations**

- **Board oversight of model risk management framework**:
  - SR 11‑7 states that the **board of directors (or a board committee) is responsible for overseeing the firm’s overall risk management framework**, including model risk management, and must **understand the bank’s reliance on models and associated limitations**.
  - The board should **approve the model risk management policy** and **review periodic reports** on model risk, including model inventory, validation findings, and remediation status.

- **Ensure independent model validation and challenge**:
  - The board must ensure management has established **independent model validation** and effective challenge processes, with sufficient stature and resources.

**Committee allocation**

- Often the **Risk Committee** is explicitly tasked with model risk oversight; the **Audit Committee** may focus on models used in financial reporting and allowance (e.g., CECL).

### 4.6 Third‑party risk management

**Key sources**

- **OCC third‑party risk management guidance** (bulletins).
- **Interagency statements** on third‑party relationships and outsourcing.
- FRB/FDIC guidance on vendor risk, cloud services, fintech partnerships.

**Representative board obligations**

- **Approve third‑party risk management framework and significant relationships**:
  - Guidance generally expects the **board to approve the overall third‑party risk management framework**, including risk appetite for outsourcing, and to **approve or be informed of material third‑party relationships**, particularly those critical to operations.

- **Oversight and reporting**:
  - The board or risk committee should receive **regular reporting on concentration risk, performance and risk of critical third parties, key contract terms, and exit strategies**.
  - The board must ensure that management has **due diligence, ongoing monitoring, and contingency planning** processes in place.

**Committee allocation**

- Typically the **Risk Committee**; sometimes combined with **Technology Committee** (if the board has one).

### 4.7 Incentive compensation

**Key sources**

- **Interagency Guidance on Sound Incentive Compensation Policies (2010)** – FRB, OCC, FDIC, and others.
- FRB SR letters implementing incentive‑comp expectations for large banking organizations.
- OCC Heightened Standards (risk governance includes compensation practices aligned with risk appetite).

**Representative board obligations**

- **Board responsibility for compensation governance**:
  - Interagency guidance places responsibility on the **board of directors to oversee the firm’s incentive compensation system**, including approving compensation policies, reviewing effectiveness, and ensuring alignment with safety and soundness.
  - The board, often through a **Compensation Committee composed largely of independent directors**, must **review incentive plans for senior executives and employees whose activities could materially affect the bank’s risk profile**.

- **Risk adjustment and deferral mechanisms**:
  - Board/Compensation Committee must ensure compensation practices include **risk adjustment, deferral, and clawback mechanisms** consistent with risk outcomes and long‑term performance.

**Committee allocation and director distinctions**

- **Compensation Committee** must typically be composed of **independent directors** (stock exchange listing standards and often bank policy).
- The **full board** retains ultimate accountability.

### 4.8 Cybersecurity governance

**Key sources**

- Interagency FFIEC Cybersecurity Assessment Tool and related handbooks.
- OCC/FRB/FDIC statements on cyber risk and information security.
- Interagency guidelines for information security (e.g., GLBA security guidelines).

**Representative board obligations**

- **Oversight of information security program**:
  - Under GLBA security guidelines (implemented by prudential regulators), the **board or a designated board committee must approve the institution’s written information security program**, oversee its implementation, and review reports on its status.
  - The board is expected to **understand cyber risk** and to ensure management has appropriate resources, staffing, and governance structures.

- **Incident response and reporting**:
  - Board must oversee management’s **incident response and breach notification processes** and receive timely briefings on significant cyber events.

**Committee allocation**

- Often a **Risk Committee** or **Technology/Cyber Committee**; the **Audit Committee** may review controls and IT general controls relevant to financial reporting.

### 4.9 BSA/AML program oversight

**Key sources**

- **Bank Secrecy Act and implementing regulations** (31 CFR Chapter X).
- **Interagency BSA/AML Examination Manual**.
- OCC, FRB, and FDIC BSA/AML guidance.

**Representative board obligations**

- **Approval of BSA/AML program**:
  - BSA regulations require banks to develop and maintain a **written BSA/AML compliance program approved by the bank’s board of directors**; the program must be noted in the board minutes.[BSA regulations and manuals require formal board approval of the BSA/AML compliance program.]

- **Appointment of BSA Officer**:
  - The board must **designate a qualified BSA Officer** with sufficient authority and resources and ensure that officer has direct access to the board or a dedicated board committee.

- **Oversight and reporting**:
  - The board or audit/risk committee must **receive regular BSA/AML reports**, including SAR filings, compliance testing results, and remediation plans, and is responsible for ensuring corrective actions are taken.

**Committee allocation**

- Typically **Audit Committee** (for compliance program oversight) or **Risk Committee**; sometimes a combined **Compliance Committee** at board level.

### 4.10 Consumer compliance oversight (CFPB and prudential regulators)

**Key sources**

- **CFPB Supervision and Examination Manual** – CMS module.
- Prudential regulators’ consumer compliance manuals (for IDIs supervised by OCC/FDIC/FRB).
- Statutes and regulations (TILA, ECOA, EFTA, etc.)—board obligations flow through CMS.

**Representative board obligations**

- **Responsibility for CMS and compliance culture**:
  - CFPB’s CMS framework states that the **board of directors is ultimately responsible for developing and overseeing the institution’s compliance management system**, including setting a culture that promotes compliance.
  - The board must **approve compliance policies, ensure resources, and receive reports** on compliance performance, customer complaints, and enforcement or exam findings.

- **Oversight of remediation**:
  - The board (or risk/compliance committee) must ensure that **compliance issues identified in exams or internal reviews are remediated** and that root causes are addressed.

**Committee allocation**

- Many GSIBs have a **Board Compliance Committee** or assign this to the **Risk Committee**; the **full board** remains accountable.

### 4.11 General safety and soundness, corporate governance, and board structure

**Key sources**

- Interagency safety and soundness guidelines (OCC part 30 App. A/B; FDIC part 364 App. A/B).
- OCC Heightened Standards for large banks (board duties, risk committee, independent risk management).[Appendix D]
- FRB CBEM § 5000.1.[2]

**Representative board obligations**

- **Maintain a safe and sound institution**:
  - The board is responsible for **ensuring policies, procedures, and internal controls are adequate** to identify, measure, monitor, and control risks; regulators evaluate whether the board meets this standard during exams.[2][OCC part 30 App. A]

- **Board composition and committee structure**:
  - Heightened Standards and Reg YY require a **board‑level risk committee** for large BHCs/GSIBs, typically chaired by an independent director and composed mostly of independent directors with risk expertise.
  - **Audit Committee**: must oversee internal controls, financial reporting, and internal audit; often must be composed entirely of independent directors (stock exchange and SOX rules, plus bank‑specific guidance).
  - **Compensation Committee**: independent directors; oversight of incentive comp and alignment with risk.
  - **Nominating/Governance Committee**: often required by listing standards; responsible for board evaluation, director independence determinations, and governance documents.

- **Inside vs outside/independent directors**:
  - Regulators expect that **risk, audit, and compensation committees** be composed largely or entirely of **independent (non‑management) directors**; independence criteria flow from securities and listing standards, as well as OCC Heightened Standards’ emphasis on independent directors for national banks’ risk committees.

## 5. Director‑type distinctions (independent vs non‑independent)

While most bank‑specific regulations do not spell out detailed independence criteria, they rely on:

- **Exchange listing standards** (NYSE/Nasdaq): require audit and compensation committees comprised entirely of independent directors, with specific financial literacy/expertise for audit committee members.
- **OCC Heightened Standards**: require the risk committee of a large national bank to include **at least one member with risk management experience** and to be chaired by an independent director; the majority of risk committee members should be independent.
- **FRB expectations**: for large BHCs and GSIBs, FRB expects **independent oversight** of risk and audit functions, which translates into independent board committees.

Practically, for Category I GSIBs, this means:

- **Audit Committee**: independent directors only; financial expertise requirement.
- **Risk Committee**: majority independent; at least one director with significant risk management experience.
- **Compensation Committee**: independent directors only.
- **Nominating/Governance Committee**: independent directors only (per listing standards).

These distinctions matter in your inventory because any obligation assigned to one of these committees effectively becomes an obligation of **independent directors** in their committee capacity, even though the full board is ultimately responsible.

## 6. Turning this into a 40–60 page inventory

To get to the level of granularity you requested, you can structure the inventory as follows:

1. **Part I – Governance architecture and cross‑cutting duties**
   - A. Full board obligations (fiduciary, safety‑and‑soundness, policy approval, capital and liquidity adequacy, compliance).
   - B. Board committees: charters and legal mandates.
     - 1. Audit Committee – statutory, regulatory, and guidance‑based duties.
     - 2. Risk Committee – duties under Reg YY, OCC Heightened Standards, and guidance.
     - 3. Compensation Committee – incentive comp and independence requirements.
     - 4. Nominating/Governance Committee – board composition, independence, evaluation.
   - C. Director types and independence expectations.

2. **Part II – Domain‑specific inventories**
   For each domain (capital planning, liquidity, resolution, recovery, operational resilience, model risk, third‑party risk, incentive comp, cyber, BSA/AML, consumer compliance, general safety and soundness), use the following grid:

   | Domain | Obligation | Source (exact cite) | Applies to (Board/Committee) | Director type (if relevant) | Notes/interpretation |
   |--------|-----------|---------------------|------------------------------|-----------------------------|----------------------|
   | Capital planning | Board must review and approve annual capital plan and ensure capital adequacy under stress | 12 CFR part 252 (capital plan rule); SR 15‑18 | Full Board (can delegate initial review to Risk Committee) | All directors; independent directors expected to provide “credible challenge” | Summarize key language and supervisory expectations |

   Each obligation should:
   - Quote or closely paraphrase the operative language.
   - Identify whether it is **binding rule** vs **supervisory guidance**.
   - Explain how examiners use the provision to evaluate board performance.

3. **Part III – Agency‑specific overlays (FRB, OCC, FDIC, CFPB)**
   - Note any differences between obligations for the BHC vs IDI subsidiaries.

   - Map obligations to sample board/committee agendas and reporting packages.
   - Identify areas of overlapping or conflicting expectations.

## 7. Key sources and why they are relevant

Below is a concise list of the most important sources for your project, with why each is central. You will need to pull the full text of each for a complete inventory.

1. **FRB Commercial Bank Examination Manual, Section 5000.1 – “Duties and Responsibilities of Directors”**[2]  
   - Explains core responsibilities of bank boards: capital adequacy, policy setting, supervision of management, understanding of condition, and compliance with laws. Foundational for all other obligations.

2. **OCC Heightened Standards – 12 CFR part 30, Appendix D**  
   - Codifies **risk governance framework** and **board of directors’ oversight** requirements for large national banks and FSBs, including board approval of risk appetite, credible challenge, and risk committee duties.

3. **Interagency Safety and Soundness Standards – OCC 12 CFR part 30 Appendix A/B; FDIC 12 CFR part 364 Appendix A/B**  
   - Provide baseline internal control, information systems, internal audit, and risk management expectations that the board must oversee.

4. **FRB Regulation YY (12 CFR part 252)**  
   - Enhanced prudential standards for large BHCs and GSIBs, including capital planning, liquidity risk management, and risk‑committee requirements; implies and sometimes states explicit board responsibilities.

5. **FRB Regulation Q (12 CFR part 217) and TLAC rules (12 CFR part 252 subpart G)**[5][8]  
   - Implement capital requirements, GSIB surcharge, and TLAC/LTD obligations; the board must oversee compliance and integration into capital and resolution strategies.

6. **FRB SR letters on capital planning (e.g., SR 15‑18, SR 15‑19)**  
   - Define supervisory expectations for board oversight of capital adequacy, capital planning, and stress testing.

7. **FRB SR 11‑7 – Model Risk Management Guidance**  
   - Establishes model risk governance framework and board oversight duties for models used across risk, capital, and financial reporting.

8. **Interagency Guidance on Sound Incentive Compensation Policies (2010)**  
   - Sets the board’s responsibilities for approving and overseeing incentive compensation systems, risk alignment, and deferral/clawback mechanisms.

9. **BSA/AML regulations and Interagency BSA/AML Examination Manual**  
   - Require formal **board approval** of BSA/AML programs and designation of a BSA officer, plus regular board reporting on AML risks and compliance.

10. **CFPB Supervision and Examination Manual – CMS module**  
    - Clarifies that the **board of directors is responsible for the compliance management system**, including policies, training, monitoring, and complaint management.

11. **OCC, FRB, FDIC guidance on third‑party risk management, cybersecurity, operational resilience**  
    - Each articulates explicit board responsibilities for approving frameworks, overseeing risk reports, and holding management accountable.

12. **FSB G‑SIB framework and TLAC standards**[3][4][6][8]  
    - Provide global context for GSIB resolvability, higher supervisory expectations, and TLAC, which U.S. rules adapt and enforce; useful for explaining why supervisory expectations for GSIB boards are higher.

13. **Industry and academic analyses (e.g., BPI paper on board role in effective governance)**[7]  
    - Helpful secondary sources summarizing and synthesizing board governance requirements for large banks, providing interpretive guidance and benchmarking.
